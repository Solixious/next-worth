What Is Net Worth — and Why Does It Matter?

Net worth is the single most important number in personal finance. It tells you exactly where you stand by subtracting everything you owe from everything you own.

The Formula

Net Worth = Total Assets − Total Liabilities

A positive net worth means your assets exceed your debts. Growing it consistently over time is the foundation of long-term financial security.

How to Calculate Your Net Worth — Step by Step

1

List all your assets

Write down everything you own that has monetary value — savings account balance, FDs, mutual fund portfolio, stocks, real estate market value, PPF/EPF corpus, gold, and vehicle resale value.

2

Add up the total asset value

Sum every asset. Don't use purchase price — use the current market or liquidation value. For real estate, use a realistic resale estimate, not the price you paid.

3

List all your liabilities

Write down every outstanding debt — home loan balance, car loan balance, personal loan, education loan, credit card dues, and any informal borrowings.

4

Add up the total liabilities

Sum the outstanding principal on every debt. Use the current outstanding balance, not the original loan amount.

5

Subtract liabilities from assets

Net Worth = Total Assets − Total Liabilities. The result is your net worth. A positive number means you own more than you owe. A negative number means the reverse — but that's fixable.

What Counts as Assets and Liabilities?

Use the net worth calculator to enter everything you own and everything you owe. Here's what typically goes in each column.

Assets — What You Own

  • Savings & current account balances
  • Fixed deposits (FDs)
  • Mutual funds & SIP portfolio value
  • Stocks & equity holdings
  • Real estate (market value)
  • PPF, EPF, NPS corpus
  • Gold, jewellery & other valuables
  • Vehicle (resale value)

Liabilities — What You Owe

  • Home loan outstanding balance
  • Car loan outstanding balance
  • Personal loan balance
  • Education loan balance
  • Credit card dues
  • Buy-now-pay-later (BNPL) balances
  • Loans from family or friends
  • Any other outstanding debt
